chapter 009　　　　　　　　　　IX  

After Inako returned to Fushimi, she learned through word of mouth that Kihachi had dropped out of middle school in Kyoto. Then, come autumn, she heard that a Buddhist monk willing to travel to the Canadian port town previously sought by the head parishioner of Zenshoku-ji had finally been found. The moment she heard this, Inako felt certain of Kihachi’s destination—and yet, anxiety coiled deep within her heart.  

In September, a large-scale riot had broken out in Vancouver’s Japantown. The cause, apparently, was local workers’ growing sense of crisis over Japanese laborers working diligently for low wages.  

“There are still those in this world who think it’s only natural to snatch another man’s rice cake.”  

Jinbei’emon muttered in outrage as he read the newspaper report about the riot.  

Even after Kihachi’s departure, the Meiji era surged forward, relentless and swift.  

The following year, in Meiji 41 (1908), sixteen white-painted warships—known collectively as the “Great White Fleet”—arrived in Yokohama. Sent by America on a round-the-world voyage to flaunt its naval power, the fleet’s arrival marked a spectacle of imperial might.

---

2.

Half a century earlier, when Perry’s Black Ships had arrived, Japan had been powerless against just four vessels. But now, to welcome this great fleet, sixteen of Japan’s own pride—its main battle squadrons—had gathered. The sight of Japanese warships standing proudly shoulder-to-shoulder with those of a world power made everyone feel, deep in their hearts, that the long-cherished dream had finally come true: Japan had at last become a first-class nation.

The Meiji era surged forward with dizzying speed.

There were frenzies over clairvoyance and Halley’s Comet. Some journeyed to the South Pole; others soared through the sky in airplanes.

Domestically produced phonographs and medical X-ray machines began appearing one after another. The unequal treaties with Western nations, which had endured for half a century, were fully abolished. By this time, the country had grown so much that it could finally savor the taste of coffee.

July, Meiji 45 (1912).

Meiji ended—after an era in which everyone had run blindly, breathlessly, without pause.

August, Taishō 1 (1912).

Inako, riding the Keiden to view the *Okuribi* festival fires, was struggling with the infant in her arms. The baby thrashed about on the white *kasuri* kimono given by her elder sister, reaching insistently for the ends of Inako’s short-cropped hair.

“You can brew sake, but you can’t soothe a baby?” Noriko laughed brightly. Her own hair, once cut short five years before, had now grown back fully to its original length.

“Kiyoshi? Oh, he’s just full of energy,” Inako replied, handing Kiyoshi back to Noriko. Roku—Hyakugawa Ken’go—sat beside her, watching with gentle eyes as Noriko effortlessly calmed the child.

2.  

Noriko and Ken’go had weathered their share of trials and tribulations since then, but they were safely married, and Ken’go had become the adopted son-in-law of the Hyakugawa family. This past January, the long-awaited first child of the Hyakugawa couple—Kiyoshi—was born.

Since then, Inako had persisted doggedly, pleading with Jinbei’emon until at last she was allowed to take part in the brewery work. Of course, learning the trade seriously meant enduring exceptional harshness; every day was a struggle under Jinbei’emon’s scolding and criticism. But recently, at last, she had begun to master the work.

Jinbei’emon still showed no sign of softening his stern demeanor. Yet according to Noriko, each time Inako managed to complete another task at the brewery, the smoke from the cigarette he puffed at the main house swayed more vigorously than usual.

Enki of Zenshoku-ji and Kei, along with Bunshichi of Hōrai Butsugu-ten, were all doing well. Occasionally Inako visited them, and each time they welcomed her as warmly as if she were family.

Saki continued to dote on Stuart-san just as before. When Inako secured her position at the brewery, Saki had taken a boat out onto Inner Lake, treating her to clear sake from lidded small bottles, with *kashiwa-junjun* as the appetizer.

She’d also heard that Stuart-san’s architectural work was progressing smoothly—he was said to have designed several buildings in Osaka already.

Speaking of Osaka, just last month—while it was still the Meiji era—a tower modeled after France’s Eiffel Tower and Arc de Triomphe had been erected on the former site of the National Industrial Exhibition in Tennoji. Inako hadn’t yet seen it with her own eyes, but judging by its name, it must surely be a tower soaring high enough to reach the heavens.

---

2.

It was now easy to reach Osaka—just like that—thanks to the Keihan Electric Railway, which had opened two years ago. Two months earlier, Shijō-dōri’s road had been widened, and the Kyoto City Tram began running along it.

And then, just yesterday, the Keishin Electric Railway had launched its service, running between Kyoto Sanjō and Ōtsu Fudanotsuji along the route of the Biwa Lake Aqueduct. In the end, the *sosui* boats had not been electrified, meaning one of the prophecies in the *Denki Mokuroku* had failed to come true. But would he feel disappointed, or would he instead rejoice at seeing electricity continue to make life easier?

What was he doing now? After all, she still hadn’t even been shown the *gentō* “Twentieth Century Denki Mokuroku” yet.

“Huh… Could there really be a *torii*-shaped bonfire signal in such an odd place?”

Lost in thought, Inako turned toward the window at the sound of a fellow passenger’s voice—and there, near the base of Inariyama, a light shaped like a *torii* gate shimmered into view. She’d never heard of a fire beacon in Fushimi.

Amid the murmuring passengers, Inako felt a sudden stirring within her: *Could it be…?*

“Hey, big sister, sorry—I need to get off at the next stop, so could you go on ahead without me?”

While soothing Seiroku, Noriko replied with a gentle smile: “Got it.”

“Take care, Inako-kun,” Kengo said, his lips curling into a faint smile.

After transferring trains, Inako arrived at Inari Shrine. She dashed up the approach and reached Akubi Ōmyōjin’s sacred mound.

“It’s exactly as I thought—it’s made of electric lights.”

Before the mound stood a large wooden frame shaped like a *torii*, with electric lamps fixed tightly into every gap.

“It’s exactly as I thought—it’s made of electric lights.”  
Before the mound stood a large wooden frame shaped like a *torii*, with electric lamps fixed tightly into every gap.

Beneath that dazzling *torii*, a young man stood alone.

His unkempt hair was a shaven head left to grow out; he wore a kimono with *hakama*, a shirt sleeve peeking out.

“—Kihachi-san?”

The youth turned at Inako’s voice.

“…Kinako-san?”

Yajirō stood there, mouth agape. Inako slumped in relief upon recognizing him. She occasionally met Yajirō, but their builds were so similar she often mistook one for the other.

“Inako-san!”  
“Really, it is!”  
Suddenly, three former members of the Night Study Meeting—Itō, Mitsu, and Sadakichi—appeared from somewhere. All now bore the faces of grown men, matured and dignified.

“We talked it over with the chief priest and decided to make the *sendaka* with electric lights this year. Just gave it a try.”

“Pretty impressive, huh?” they chimed together.

Inako looked up at the *torii* again. It blazed with powerful radiance—bright enough, perhaps, to be seen even from the moon.

“Kihachi-san would’ve been able to see it, if only he’d come back from Canada by now.”

“Canada?” Yajirō tilted his head.

“You know—he went to Canada as a Buddhist monk. How much longer do we have to wait for him to return?”

The members of the Night Study Meeting exchanged uneasy glances, then Yajirō spoke gently:

“Kinako-san… it wasn’t Kihachi-san who went to Canada.  
It was Yōsuke-san.”

---

2.

According to Yajirō, after being dismissed as branch manager, Yōsuke had been sent by Sakamoto Kihachi’s orders to train at Zenshoku-ji upon hearing of a call for Buddhist monks in Canada—and was dispatched there just months later. From what they’d heard, though he was still being driven around like a parishioner’s packhorse, he was apparently adjusting surprisingly well to life overseas.

“We’ve still got business with the head priest, so we’ll leave it here. Take your time looking around.”

Leaving Inako behind, still dazed and unable to sort her thoughts, Yajirō and the others disappeared down the depths of the approach path.

Under the glow of the electric lamp, Inako reached into her purse and pulled out a photograph taken at Hachiman—Kihachi and herself standing side by side, stiff with nervousness. Where, she wondered, gazing at the photo, was the man who had turned his face away from the camera?

“Well? Pretty nice, huh?”

She turned at the voice behind her and saw a young man standing beneath the lamplight.

His hair was neatly cropped, and he wore an unfamiliar student’s uniform from head to toe.

“…Kihachi-san…?”

Taller now, with a sharper, more dignified bearing, Kihachi smiled gently. “Long time no see.”

“Where… where have you been all this time?”

“Fulfilling my promise to Jinbei’emon.”

The condition for marrying Inako, set by Jinbei’emon, had been clear: *a man who had at least graduated university*. Refusing to accept any refund, he had forced upon Kihachi a binding agreement—*attend university*. To prevent Kihachi from seeing Inako during his studies and losing focus, he’d even gone so far as to transfer him all the way to a middle school in Tokyo.

“Take a look at this high school uniform. Once I get into high school, I can enter university without taking exams.”

2.

Kihachi poked happily at the fabric of his uniform, but grew uneasy when Inako said nothing.

“Um… sorry I couldn’t contact you at all.”

Inako pulled out the *Denki Mokuroku* and said, “Entry twenty-one: *Love*.”

“When I met you again, Kihachi-san, electricity ran through me.”

Kihachi’s anxious eyes slowly widened.

“It was because you sent that current through me, Kihachi-san, that I could smile again.”

Kihachi wore a complex expression—on the verge of tears, yet smiling.

“I’ve come back safely, just like I promised,” he said, his face turning bright red.  
“From now on, please… keep being my goddess.”

“…You’re not an idiot, are you?”

“Who’s the idiot?!”

“If we’re going to be together forever, then you don’t need to be my god—you should be my *kami-san* instead.”

Kihachi gaped dumbfounded, then gave a tearful chuckle. “Clever one, aren’t you…” With that, he gently ran his fingers through Inako’s hair. She placed her hand over his.

“Your hair’s still in the Twentieth Century style. And that white *kasuri* kimono suits you perfectly.”

“Of course. It’s the hairstyle and kimono you once said looked so pretty.”

“…I made you wait five whole years. I’m amazed you stayed faithful all this time.”

Inako offered him the very same smile he’d once called adorable.

“Believing in people is what I do best.”

And Kihachi, too, crumpled his face into a wide, beaming grin.

“I believed too.”

**Afterword**  
I think it was during high school when I first watched an anime by Kyoto Animation. Until then, I’d always imagined anime being made somewhere distant and abstract—but seeing the word “Kyoto” sparked a sudden sense of closeness. That such a studio would now publish a story I myself created feels like a dream come true.

From my teenage years onward, there was a time when I struggled—unable to do things others found easy, uninterested in what everyone else enjoyed, lost constantly in daydreams, tormented by feeling unable to be ordinary.  
I feared I’d end up forever as just a strange person with an obsession for fantasy.  
When that thought struck me, I decided: if I can’t live ordinarily, then I’ll compete with the world through my strangeness instead—and thus began writing stories.

Later, when someone described a novel I wrote for friends as “too immature,” I didn’t try to fix it. Instead, I smirked and thought, *Exactly—this is something only I can create right now, so I’ll push this rawness to its limit.* Even when submitting this work set in the Meiji era, I felt less worried about “breaking category expectations” than excited by the thought: *What kind of reaction will this provoke?*

As for this work itself, the forbidden shrine featured in the main story has a real-life model. I’ve packed this tale tightly with things I’ve actually seen or heard, things I’ve reexamined, and above all, things I truly wanted to convey. How have readers received this story? If even a little of it has left a mark on your heart, I would be delighted.

Having passed the halfway point, I’d now like to express my gratitude to everyone who has supported me thus far.
