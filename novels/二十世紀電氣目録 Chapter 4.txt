As the tram approached Okazaki’s Nyōōmon-dōri, the vermilion-painted Ōtenmon gate of Heian Shrine came into view far to the left. Inako and Yajirō pressed their faces close to the window, gazing toward the city’s memorial zoo, opened four years prior.

Between Nyōōmon-dōri and the zoo flowed the Biwa Lake Aqueduct. To the east, it led to Nanzen-ji Temple’s boat basin. From the basin, the Keage Incline stretched straight upstream to the upper boat basin at Keage.

2.

“Huh, this is the first time I’ve seen an incline other than Sumizome.”

The Biwa Lake Aqueduct, constructed to link Lake Biwa and Kyoto with the aim of advancing water-powered industry, had been completed in Meiji 23 (1890). Utilizing the aqueduct, a hydroelectric power station began operations, supplying electricity that powered Japan’s first commercial electric railway, as well as industrial machinery and electric lighting. This became a major driving force behind Kyoto’s revival—a city that had been steadily declining since the capital’s relocation to Tokyo.

“Seeing the incline makes me think of the water chute at the National Industrial Exhibition.”

Kihachi narrowed his eyes fondly. The water chute had been one of the attractions at the exhibition—an amusement where boats carrying passengers slid down a steep slope and splashed dramatically into a pool below. However, the ride cost twenty sen, which was quite expensive, so Kihachi and Seiroku had apparently watched it from the precincts of Chaushiyama Temple.

The electric tram carrying the three of them climbed upward along the Ōtō Extension Line, which had just opened this month and ran alongside the incline.

On the adjacent incline, a boat mounted on a cart was being slowly winched upward by steel cables. A boatman, casually seated atop the cargo, leisurely puffed on his kiseru pipe. Along the mountainside, the dignified, hybrid Japanese-Western buildings of the Miyako Hotel stood in a row, their white walls standing out vividly against the surrounding greenery.

After disembarking, the trio boarded a *sosui* boat at Keage’s boat basin. The boat began to move forward slowly.

After passing through the longest tunnel on the Biwa Lake Aqueduct, they had entered Shiga Prefecture.

Especially the flood eleven years ago was one of the largest in recorded history. Centered on farms whose rice crops had been completely wiped out before harvest, coastal villages suffered catastrophic damage—so severe that some people decided then and there to emigrate overseas.

Yōsuke’s relatives, whose business had been disrupted by the flood, resolved to move to Canada. At the time, Yōsuke was a young man, and due to family decisions, he left for Canada with his younger sister, accompanying the relatives to gain commercial experience and broaden his horizons.

In those days, traveling abroad was not particularly rare; many Japanese were migrating to Hawaii or California. Around the time of the flood, entering Hawaii required a substantial sum of one hundred yen, so most emigrants from Shiga Prefecture chose instead to settle in areas like Vancouver, Canada.

Unlike other migrants who worked in forestry or fishing, Yōsuke’s relatives had a certain amount of capital. They opened a shop in Vancouver selling goods imported from Japan: chirimen crepe silk, hemp cloth, bamboo-root whip (*takeyukibuchi*) crafts, kuzu crafts, and sake.

Like many Japanese laborers who went abroad hoping “to return home in glory,” the relatives eventually earned significant profits. After entrusting their store to an acquaintance, they returned once more to Ōmi. But Yōsuke did not return with them. Instead, determined to test his own mercantile talents, he boldly struck out into the wider world.

He would import Japanese goods popular among foreigners, sell them in the countries he visited, use the proceeds to purchase local alcoholic beverages, then ship and sell those in Japan. With the profits from that, he’d travel to another country, buy its local liquor, and repeat the cycle.
